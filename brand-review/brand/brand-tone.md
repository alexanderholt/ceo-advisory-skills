# Desata Brand Tone Guide

**Version:** March 2026

---

## Who We're Talking To

Financial aid officers, financial aid directors, and compliance staff at Title IV institutions. These are people who know federal student aid policy inside and out. They've read the regs. They've sat through the Dear Colleague Letters. They don't need things dumbed down, and they can smell marketing from a mile away.

Secondary audience: lenders and loan servicers evaluating ClarifiED as a distribution channel.

---

## The Voice

Desata sounds like a senior colleague who happens to have built something useful. Not a vendor. Not a startup trying to disrupt anything. Someone who was in the room when the policy got written and built the tools because nobody else was going to.

Direct. Precise. Collegial. The kind of person you'd trust to explain a reg change in plain language without oversimplifying it.

---

## What We Sound Like

- A policy expert explaining something clearly to a peer
- A financial aid director recommending a tool to another director over email
- Someone who knows the difference between unsubsidized and Grad PLUS and doesn't need to look it up

## What We Don't Sound Like

- A SaaS landing page ("Revolutionize your workflow!")
- A press release ("We are pleased to announce...")
- A consultant who just learned what OBBBA stands for last week
- Anything with exclamation points, buzzwords, or stock photography energy

---

## Specific Guidelines

**Use the correct terms.** Say "schedule of reduction," not "proration" if that's what the reg says. Say "preferred lender list," not "lender directory." The audience knows the terminology and will notice if you get it wrong.

**Don't oversell.** If something is in development, say it's in development. If something is free, say it's free — once. Don't repeat it three times in bold.

**Don't name-drop competitors aggressively.** We can reference the category ("unlike commission-based platforms") without calling out Credible or NerdWallet by name on our public-facing materials. The one-pager for lenders can be more direct because that audience expects competitive positioning.

**Explain the "why" before the "what."** The audience cares about compliance risk, regulatory deadlines, and student outcomes. Lead with the problem they're already dealing with, then show how the tool addresses it.

**No fluff paragraphs.** If a sentence doesn't add information, cut it. If a section header can do the work alone, let it.

**Be honest about what we don't know.** If the final rule hasn't been published, say that. If a feature isn't built yet, say that. This audience respects candor and will distrust anything that feels like it's getting ahead of itself.

---

## Tone by Context

**Website (desata.io):** Clean, informational, peer-to-peer. Every page should work as a standalone link that an FAO can forward to a colleague. No scrolling through marketing to find the point.

**Lender one-pager:** Slightly more assertive. This is a sales document, but it earns trust by being specific and transparent about the model (flat fees, no commissions, published methodology). The competitive framing is direct but not aggressive.

**Investor materials (deck, memo, model):** Confident and data-driven. The audience is VCs — they want market size, unit economics, and defensibility. The tone can be bolder here, but the numbers need to be airtight. Don't claim what you can't back up.

**Email and outreach:** Short. Respectful of time. One ask per email. No "just circling back" or "hope this finds you well." Get to the point.

## Words We Use

- Recommendation engine (not marketplace, not aggregator)
- No-Influence ratings
- Access, Affordability, Flexibility (the three criteria)
- Flat annual access fee (not subscription, not SaaS fee)
- PLL-compliant (not "compliant with preferred lender list regulations")
- Financial aid office / FAO (not "institution" or "university partner")
- Borrower (not "customer" or "user" when talking about students)
- Schedule of reduction (not proration, when referring to the OBBBA loan limit rules)

## Words We Avoid

- Disrupt / disruption
- Revolutionary / game-changing
- Leverage (as a verb)
- Solution (when we mean "tool")
- Best-in-class
- Seamless (unless describing a specific technical integration)
- Any exclamation points in professional copy
