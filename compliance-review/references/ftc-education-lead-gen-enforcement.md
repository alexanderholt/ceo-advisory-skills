## Overview

The FTC and CFPB have taken multiple enforcement actions that define the boundary between legitimate comparison/recommendation tools and regulated (or deceptive) lead generation. These cases are relevant to Desata because ClarifiED operates in the space between a comparison tool and a lead generator — and the regulatory distinction between the two has significant consequences.

## Key Enforcement Actions

### FTC v. Expand Inc. d/b/a Gigats (2016)

**Case:** FTC File No. 142-3243
**Defendants:** Expand Inc., doing business as Gigats.com, EducationMatch, and SoftRock Inc.; CEO Ayman A. Difrawi
**Date:** April 2016

**What happened:**
- Gigats.com scraped job listings from the internet and presented them on its website as if it were accepting job applications on behalf of employers
- Consumers submitted personal information (name, address, phone, education history) believing they were applying for jobs
- Employers had **not authorized** Gigats to collect applications or screen applicants. Many job postings were not current.
- Consumers who submitted information were directed to call "employment specialists" who were actually **education advisors** employed by Gigats
- These advisors steered consumers toward education programs that **paid Gigats for leads**, typically $22-$125 per lead
- Advisors falsely claimed to be independent and to recommend the "best" programs, when in fact they only recommended programs that paid Gigats

**Violations:**
- Deceptive practices under Section 5 of the FTC Act
- Misrepresentation of the nature and purpose of data collection
- Failure to disclose the commercial relationship between Gigats and the schools it recommended

**Settlement:**
- $90.2 million judgment (suspended upon payment of $360,000 based on financial inability)
- Prohibition on future misrepresentations about data collection purposes
- Prohibition on transferring consumer information to third parties without clear disclosure
- Requirement for affirmative consumer consent before using data

**Key principle established:** A lead generator that presents itself as serving consumers' interests (job matching, education recommendations) while actually steering consumers to paying clients commits a deceptive practice. The undisclosed financial relationship is the core violation.

### FTC v. LoanPointe LLC (2010-2011)

**Case:** FTC File No. 102-3021
**Defendants:** Eastbrook LLC (d/b/a Ecash, GeteCash), LoanPointe LLC, Joe S. Strom, and others
**Date:** Complaint filed April 2010; final order December 2011

**What happened:**
- LoanPointe and related entities offered payday loans of up to $1,000 online
- Loan applications required borrowers to check a box agreeing to loan terms that included an **inconspicuous wage garnishment clause** that most borrowers did not notice
- When borrowers were delinquent, the defendants attempted to garnish wages based on this buried consent

**Violations:**
- Deceptive practices (misrepresenting loan terms)
- Violations of the Truth in Lending Act
- Violations of the Credit Practices Rule
- Violations of the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act

**Settlement:**
- Court ordered defendants to pay $294,536
- Prohibited from misrepresenting loan terms, rates, conditions, or amounts
- Prohibited from violating TILA, Credit Practices Rule, and FDCPA

**Key principle established:** Burying material terms in fine print or behind pre-checked consent boxes is deceptive. Material terms of financial products must be clearly and conspicuously disclosed.

### FTC "Follow the Lead" Workshop (October 2015)

**Source:** https://www.ftc.gov/reports/follow-lead-workshop-staff-perspective

The FTC hosted this workshop to examine online lead generation across industries including education and lending. The subsequent FTC Staff Perspective report identified key principles:

**What lead generation is:**
Lead generation is the practice of identifying or cultivating consumer interest in a product or service, and distributing this information (the "lead") to third parties. Leads often contain sensitive personal and financial information that may pass through multiple marketing entities before reaching the end business.

**Key regulatory concerns identified:**
1. **Transparency of data collection.** Consumers often do not understand that their information is being collected for lead generation purposes, or how many entities will receive it.
2. **Deceptive framing.** Lead generators that present themselves as neutral comparison tools, job sites, or advisory services — while actually monetizing consumer data through lead sales — engage in deceptive practices.
3. **Data security.** Consumer data passing through multiple intermediaries increases exposure to data breaches.
4. **Consent.** The FTC expects clear, affirmative consent for data sharing, not buried disclosures or pre-checked boxes.

**Distinction between legitimate comparison and lead generation:**
The workshop materials drew a line between:
- **Legitimate comparison tools** that present product information to help consumers make informed choices, funded by transparent business models
- **Lead generators** that collect consumer data under the guise of helping consumers, then sell that data to the highest bidder

## CFPB Circular 2024-01 — Comparison-Shopping Tools

**Date:** February 29, 2024
**Source:** https://www.consumerfinance.gov/compliance/circulars/consumer-financial-protection-circular-2024-01/

While not an FTC action, this CFPB circular directly addresses the comparison-tool space and is the most current regulatory guidance on the topic.

**Key findings:**

1. **Operators of comparison-shopping tools may be "covered persons"** subject to CFPB jurisdiction under the Consumer Financial Protection Act.

2. **Steering is abusive.** Where consumers reasonably rely on an operator of a comparison-shopping tool to act in their interests, the operator can take **unreasonable advantage** of that reliance by:
   - Giving preferential placement to products based on compensation rather than consumer benefit
   - Using dynamic bidding systems or volume allocation practices that manipulate product presentation
   - Labeling paid placements as "featured" or "recommended" without disclosing the financial relationship

3. **Lead generators face the same standard.** Lead generators that steer consumers to one provider over another based on compensation — rather than fit — may violate the prohibition on abusive practices.

4. **The test is consumer reliance.** If consumers reasonably believe the tool is acting in their interest (e.g., presenting "best" or "recommended" options), any undisclosed financial incentive that distorts the results is potentially abusive.

## The Line Between Comparison Tools and Lead Generation

Synthesizing across these enforcement actions and guidance, the regulatory distinction comes down to:

| Factor | Legitimate Comparison Tool | Regulated/Deceptive Lead Generation |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue model** | Transparent; not per-lead | Per-lead payments from providers |
| **Data flow** | Product info flows to consumers | Consumer data flows to providers |
| **Recommendations** | Based on objective criteria, publicly disclosed | Based on which provider pays most |
| **Consumer understanding** | Consumer knows the tool's business model | Consumer believes tool is neutral |
| **Consent** | Clear disclosure of what happens with data | Buried or absent disclosure |
| **Branding** | Honest about role and relationships | Poses as neutral advisor, job site, etc. |

## Relevance to Desata

ClarifiED's design positions it on the **legitimate comparison tool** side of this line:

1. **No per-lead compensation.** Flat annual fee per institution eliminates the steering incentive.
2. **Published methodology.** Transparent ranking criteria that institutions and regulators can audit.
3. **Data flows from lenders to borrowers,** not borrower data to lenders.
4. **No-influence by design.** The recommendation engine does not accept payments from lenders to influence placement.

**Risk areas to monitor:**
- If any feature allows lenders to pay for enhanced visibility or placement, the tool crosses into lead-generation territory
- If the tool collects borrower data and shares it with lenders (even for "pre-qualification"), it moves toward lead generation
- Marketing claims that ClarifiED "recommends the best" loans create consumer reliance — the methodology must genuinely support that claim
