## Overview

The SLATE Act is New York's state-level preferred lender list law, enacted in response to the 2007 student lending scandals (triggered in part by NY Attorney General Andrew Cuomo's investigation). It is widely considered the **strictest state PLL law** in the country. It predates (and in some respects exceeds) the federal HEOA PLL requirements enacted in 2008.

---

## Key Provisions

### Preferred Lender List Requirements (§679-c)

If an institution chooses to maintain a preferred lender list, it must comply with the following:

1. **Best interest standard:** The institution's decision to include a lender on the preferred lender list, and the placement/location of the lender on the list, must be determined **solely by consideration of the best interest of the borrowers** and without regard to the monetary interests of the institution.

2. **Disclosure of process:** The preferred lender list must disclose:
   - The process by which the institution selected lenders for the list
   - The method and criteria used to choose the lenders
   - The relative importance of the criteria used

3. **Borrower choice notice:** The preferred lender list must state — **in the same font size and manner as the predominant text on the document** — that:
   - Borrowers have the right and ability to select the lender of their choice
   - Borrowers do not have to choose a lender on the list
   - Borrowers will suffer no penalty for choosing a lender not on the list

4. **Minimum number of lenders:** The list must include a minimum number of lenders that are not affiliates of each other (consistent with federal requirements of at least 3 unaffiliated lenders for FFEL, and practice of at least 3 for private loans).

5. **Annual review:** The preferred lender list must be reviewed and updated at least annually.

### Prohibited Practices (§679-d)

The SLATE Act prohibits:

1. **Revenue sharing:** No institution may enter into any revenue-sharing arrangement with any lender.

2. **Gifts and trips:** No employee of an institution who has responsibilities with respect to education loans may solicit or accept any gift from a lender, guarantor, or servicer.

3. **Staffing arrangements:** No institution may enter into any arrangement with a lender under which the lender places employees or agents in the institution's financial aid office or otherwise engages in financial aid activities at the institution.

4. **Advisory board compensation:** No employee of an institution who has responsibilities with respect to education loans may receive anything of value from a lender or affiliate in exchange for serving on an advisory board, commission, or group established by the lender (except reimbursement for reasonable expenses).

5. **Steering:** No institution may assign a borrower's loan to a particular lender, or refuse to certify or delay certification of a loan based on the borrower's choice of lender.

6. **Quid pro quo:** No lender may be placed on an institution's preferred lender list, or in a favored location on the list, for a particular type of loan **in exchange for benefits provided to the institution or to the institution's students in connection with a different type of loan**.

### Institutional Code of Conduct (§679-e)

Each institution must develop, publish, and administer a code of conduct governing its relationship with lenders. The code must:
- Be prominently posted on the institution's website
- Be distributed to all employees with financial aid responsibilities
- Prohibit conflicts of interest
- Ban revenue-sharing arrangements
- Ban gifts
- Require disclosure of any arrangements with lenders

### Enforcement

- NY Attorney General has enforcement authority
- The AG may investigate and bring actions for violations
- Cuomo's office was notably aggressive in enforcement during 2007-2008, resulting in settlements with multiple institutions and lenders

## Key Differences from Federal HEOA PLL Requirements

| Provision | NY SLATE Act | Federal HEOA (34 C.F.R. Part 601) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------------|
| Enacted | 2007 | 2008 |
| Best interest standard | Explicit statutory requirement | Similar but less detailed |
| Revenue sharing ban | Absolute prohibition | Prohibited |
| Gift prohibition | Broad — any gift from lender | Broader exceptions for certain items |
| Font size requirement | Must match predominant text | No specific font requirement |
| Quid pro quo ban | Explicit — no cross-product deal-making | Not as explicitly addressed |
| Enforcement | State AG with active enforcement history | ED (via institutional participation agreements) |
| Advisory board restrictions | Cannot receive "anything of value" (except expenses) | Similar but slightly less restrictive |

## ClarifiED Relevance

1. **Not a lender, but implications remain:** ClarifiED is not a lender, but it facilitates loan comparisons. If an institution uses ClarifiED's recommendations as a preferred lender list or quasi-PLL, the SLATE Act requirements apply to the institution's use of those recommendations.

2. **Best interest standard:** ClarifiED's published methodology and no-commission model directly support compliance with the SLATE Act's requirement that PLL decisions be based solely on borrower best interest.

3. **Revenue sharing prohibition:** ClarifiED's flat fee model (no commissions, no lender payments) avoids the revenue-sharing prohibition.

4. **Steering prohibition:** ClarifiED must not assign borrowers to particular lenders or create the appearance of assignment. The borrower must retain full choice.

5. **Disclosure requirements:** If NY institutions use ClarifiED, they must still disclose selection methodology, criteria, and relative importance — ClarifiED's published methodology supports this.
