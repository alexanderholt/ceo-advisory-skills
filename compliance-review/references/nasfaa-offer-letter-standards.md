## Overview

There is no single federal mandate requiring a standardized financial aid offer letter format (though legislation has been proposed). Instead, the industry operates under a combination of:

1. **The College Financing Plan (CFP)** — a voluntary federal template from the Department of Education
2. **The College Cost Transparency Initiative (CCT)** — a voluntary set of principles and standards from a coalition of higher education associations led by NASFAA
3. **The NASFAA Glossary of Terms** — consensus definitions for financial aid terminology

Together, these represent the closest thing to industry-standard offer letter requirements.

## The College Financing Plan (CFP)

### History
- Originally called the **Financial Aid Shopping Sheet**, introduced in 2012 by the Obama administration
- Voluntary adoption — institutions could choose to use it as their primary or supplementary communication to students
- Renamed the **College Financing Plan** and revised for the 2019-20 award year
- Updated annually by the Department of Education (most recent: 2024-25)

### What It Includes
The CFP template is designed to provide students with a standardized view of:

1. **Cost of Attendance (COA)** — itemized into:
   - Tuition and fees
   - Housing and meals
   - Books and supplies
   - Transportation
   - Other education costs

2. **Grants and Scholarships** (money that does not need to be repaid)
   - Federal Pell Grant
   - Institutional grants and scholarships
   - State grants and scholarships

3. **Net Price** — Cost of attendance minus total grants and scholarships

4. **Options to Pay Net Price:**
   - Work options (Federal Work-Study)
   - Loan options (federal and institutional)
   - Other options (payment plans, military/veteran benefits)

5. **Estimated Cost After Grants, Scholarships, and Work Options**

6. **Graduation Rate and Loan Default Rate** for the institution

### Adoption Rate
Per the GAO-23-104708 report (2022):
- Only about **3% of colleges** used the CFP template as their primary communication to students
- About **two-thirds** did not use the template at all
- Many institutions created their own formats with varying levels of clarity

## College Cost Transparency Initiative (CCT)

### What It Is
In fall 2022, **10 higher education associations** launched the CCT to improve the clarity, accuracy, and consistency of financial aid offers. Participating associations include organizations representing college presidents, financial aid offices, enrollment managers, and admissions counselors.

NASFAA is a lead organizer.

### Participating Associations
The founding coalition included:
- NASFAA (National Association of Student Financial Aid Administrators)
- ACE (American Council on Education)
- NACAC (National Association for College Admission Counseling)
- AACRAO (American Association of Collegiate Registrars and Admissions Officers)
- And six additional higher education associations

### Institutional Adoption
- As of September 2024, **more than 615 institutions** across the U.S. have voluntarily committed to the CCT principles and standards
- Institutions pledge to align their financial aid offers with the initiative's principles

### Key Principles and Standards

The CCT established principles that financial aid offers should:

1. **Be clear and understandable.** Use plain language and commonly understood terms.
2. **Be accurate.** Include correct cost and aid figures specific to the student.
3. **Include key cost information.** Present itemized direct costs (tuition, fees, housing, meals) and indirect costs (books, transportation, personal expenses).
4. **Distinguish gift aid from self-help aid.** Clearly separate grants/scholarships (free money) from loans (borrowed money) and work-study (earned money).
5. **Show net price.** Calculate and display cost of attendance minus grants and scholarships.
6. **Use standard terminology.** Use the NASFAA Glossary of Terms.
7. **Not conflate types of aid.** Do not lump grants and loans together in a single "financial aid package" number without differentiation.
8. **Not present loans as "awards."** Loans are options, not gifts.

## NASFAA Glossary of Terms for Financial Aid Offers

### History
- Created in 2012 by NASFAA's Award Notification Task Force
- Updated in 2019 after consultation with NASFAA membership and the broader higher education community
- Updated again in March 2024 to align with the College Cost Transparency Initiative

### Purpose
The glossary provides **universally accepted definitions** of language typically contained on financial aid offers. Its goal is consistency across institutions so students can compare offers using the same terminology.

### Key Terms and Definitions

| Term | Standard Definition |
|---|---|
| **Cost of Attendance (COA)** | The total estimated cost for a student to attend the institution for a specific period, including tuition, fees, room, board, books, supplies, transportation, and personal expenses |
| **Direct Costs** | Costs billed directly by the institution (tuition, fees, on-campus room and board) |
| **Indirect Costs** | Estimated costs not billed by the institution (books, supplies, transportation, personal expenses, off-campus housing) |
| **Net Price** | Cost of attendance minus grants and scholarships |
| **Grant** | Financial aid that does not need to be repaid; based on financial need and/or other criteria |
| **Scholarship** | Financial aid that does not need to be repaid; often based on merit, talent, or specific criteria |
| **Loan** | Borrowed money that must be repaid with interest |
| **Federal Work-Study** | A federal financial aid program that provides part-time employment to help students pay for education expenses |
| **Expected Family Contribution (EFC) / Student Aid Index (SAI)** | A measure of a family's financial strength used to determine eligibility for federal student aid (EFC replaced by SAI starting 2024-25) |
| **Unmet Need** | The difference between a student's cost of attendance and the total financial aid (grants, scholarships, loans, work-study) offered |

### Terms to Avoid

The glossary and CCT guidance recommend avoiding:
- **"Award"** when referring to loans (loans are not awards)
- **"Financial aid package"** as a single undifferentiated number (separate gift aid from self-help)
- Institutional jargon that students and families may not understand
- Ambiguous terms like "tuition discount" or "institutional aid" without clarification

## Proposed Federal Legislation

Multiple bills have been introduced in Congress to mandate standardized offer letters:

- **Understanding the True Cost of College Act** — Would require institutions to use a standardized form
- **College Transparency Act** — Would enhance data availability for comparison
- **FAFSA Simplification Act** (enacted 2020) — Changed the Student Aid Index calculation but did not mandate offer letter format

As of this writing, no federal law mandates a specific offer letter format. The NASFAA/CCT standards remain voluntary.
