## Overview

The CCPA, as amended by CPRA (Proposition 24, 2020), is California's comprehensive consumer privacy law. It grants California residents ("consumers") rights over their personal information and imposes obligations on covered businesses. The California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) has rulemaking and enforcement authority.

## Applicability — When Does CCPA Apply to ClarifiED?

### Covered Businesses (§1798.140(d))

CCPA applies to a for-profit entity doing business in California that collects consumers' personal information AND satisfies one or more of:
1. Annual gross revenues exceeding $25 million (adjusted for inflation)
2. Annually buys, sells, or shares the personal information of 100,000 or more consumers or households
3. Derives 50% or more of annual revenues from selling or sharing consumers' personal information

> **ClarifiED relevance:** Even as a pre-revenue startup, once ClarifiED exceeds any threshold (likely the $25M revenue threshold first), CCPA applies. However, even before reaching thresholds, California students' data may be handled by institution customers who are themselves covered — making ClarifiED a "service provider" subject to contractual CCPA requirements.

### Key Exemptions

- **GLBA exemption (§1798.145(e)):** Personal information collected, processed, sold, or disclosed pursuant to GLBA is exempt. If ClarifiED data is covered by GLBA, the GLBA exemption may apply to some or all of it.
- **FERPA exemption (§1798.145(g)):** Personal information collected from education records regulated by FERPA is exempt from CCPA.
- **B2B exemption:** The temporary B2B exemption (for personal information of employees, job applicants, and B2B contacts) **expired January 1, 2023**. B2B personal information is now fully covered.

> **Important:** The GLBA and FERPA exemptions are data-level, not entity-level. ClarifiED might handle some data covered by these exemptions and other data that is not exempt.

## Consumer Rights (effective as of Jan. 1, 2023, with 2026 regulatory updates)

1. **Right to Know** (§1798.100, §1798.110) — what personal information is collected, categories of sources, business purpose, categories of third parties
2. **Right to Delete** (§1798.105) — request deletion of personal information
3. **Right to Correct** (§1798.106) — request correction of inaccurate personal information
4. **Right to Opt Out of Sale/Sharing** (§1798.120) — consumers may opt out of the sale or sharing of their personal information; must honor Global Privacy Control signals
5. **Right to Limit Use of Sensitive Personal Information** (§1798.121) — consumers may direct businesses to limit use to what is necessary for the disclosed purpose
6. **Right to Non-Discrimination** (§1798.125) — businesses may not discriminate against consumers for exercising CCPA rights
7. **Right to Access Information About Automated Decision-Making** (new 2026 regulations) — consumers have the right to information about automated decision-making technology (ADMT) and, in certain cases, the right to opt out of ADMT

## Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT) — 2026 Regulations

The CPPA's 2025 rulemaking (effective Jan. 1, 2026) added requirements for businesses using ADMT:
- **Pre-use notice** about ADMT use, the logic involved, and intended output
- **Right to opt out** of certain ADMT uses
- **Right to access** information about ADMT decisions
- Businesses must conduct **risk assessments** for ADMT that involves profiling for decisions with legal or similarly significant effects

> **ClarifiED relevance:** A loan recommendation algorithm that produces recommendations affecting borrowers' access to financial products is likely subject to the ADMT provisions. This would require: pre-use notices to borrowers, an opt-out mechanism, and risk assessments.

## Service Provider Obligations (§1798.140(ag))

A "service provider" processes personal information on behalf of a business pursuant to a written contract. The contract must:
- Specify the business purpose for processing
- Prohibit the service provider from selling or sharing the personal information
- Prohibit the service provider from retaining, using, or disclosing the information for purposes other than the contracted business purpose
- Prohibit combining the personal information with data from other sources (except as permitted)
- Require the service provider to comply with CCPA and allow the business to take reasonable steps to ensure compliance
- Require the service provider to notify the business if it can no longer meet its CCPA obligations

### Cybersecurity Audit Requirements (2026 Regulations)

Businesses meeting specific thresholds must:
- Undergo independent cybersecurity audits
- Submit audit reports and executive certifications to the CPPA
- Maintain written technical and organizational security controls

### Contracts with Service Providers, Contractors, and Third Parties (2026 Regulations)

Must include:
- Specific purposes for data use
- Privacy obligations
- Full cooperation with cybersecurity audits and risk assessments
- Pass-down of obligations to subcontractors

## Enforcement

- **CPPA:** Administrative enforcement authority, can impose fines up to $2,500 per violation or $7,500 per intentional violation (and per violation involving minors)
- **CA Attorney General:** Concurrent enforcement authority
- **Private right of action:** Limited to data breaches (§1798.150) — statutory damages of $100-$750 per consumer per incident, or actual damages
