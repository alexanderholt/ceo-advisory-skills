# CAN-SPAM Act

**Citation:** Controlling the Assault of Non-Solicited Pornography and Marketing Act of 2003, 15 U.S.C. Sections 7701-7713
**Source:** https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/chapter-103
**FTC Compliance Guide:** https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business
**Implementing Rule:** 16 C.F.R. Part 316
**Date:** Signed into law December 16, 2003. Effective January 1, 2004.

---

## Overview

The CAN-SPAM Act establishes requirements for commercial electronic mail messages, gives recipients the right to stop receiving commercial emails, and imposes penalties for violations. It is enforced by the FTC and preempts most state laws regulating commercial email (with narrow exceptions for fraud and computer crime statutes).

**Important:** CAN-SPAM does not require prior consent to send commercial email. It is an opt-out regime — senders may send commercial email until the recipient opts out.

## Key Definitions (15 U.S.C. Section 7702)

### Commercial Electronic Mail Message
Any electronic mail message the **primary purpose** of which is the commercial advertisement or promotion of a commercial product or service, including content on an Internet website operated for a commercial purpose.

### Transactional or Relationship Message
A message whose primary purpose is to:
- Facilitate, complete, or confirm a commercial transaction the recipient has already agreed to
- Provide warranty, recall, safety, or security information about a product or service
- Provide information about a subscription, membership, account, loan, or similar ongoing relationship
- Deliver goods or services, including product updates or upgrades

Transactional messages are **exempt** from most CAN-SPAM requirements (but may not contain false or misleading header information).

### Sender / Initiator
The **sender** is the person who initiates or advertises in a commercial email. The **initiator** is the person who originates the message or procures its origination. Both can be held liable.

## Seven Core Requirements

The FTC distills CAN-SPAM compliance into seven requirements:

### 1. No False or Misleading Header Information
The "From," "To," "Reply-To," and routing information — including the originating domain name and email address — must be **accurate** and identify the person or business who initiated the message. (15 U.S.C. Section 7704(a)(1))

### 2. No Deceptive Subject Lines
The subject line must **accurately reflect** the content of the message. (15 U.S.C. Section 7704(a)(2))

### 3. Identify the Message as an Advertisement
The message must **clearly and conspicuously** disclose that it is an advertisement or solicitation. (15 U.S.C. Section 7704(a)(5)(A))

There is flexibility in how to make this disclosure, but it must be clear. Exception: if the recipient has given prior affirmative consent to receive the message, this requirement does not apply.

### 4. Include a Valid Physical Postal Address
The message must include the sender's **valid physical postal address**. This can be: (15 U.S.C. Section 7704(a)(5)(A))
- A current street address
- A post office box registered with the U.S. Postal Service
- A private mailbox registered with a commercial mail receiving agency under USPS regulations

### 5. Provide an Opt-Out Mechanism
The message must provide a **clear and conspicuous** explanation of how the recipient can opt out of receiving future commercial email. (15 U.S.C. Section 7704(a)(3))
- The opt-out mechanism must be able to process requests for **at least 30 days** after the message is sent
- The opt-out must be **free of charge** to the recipient
- The opt-out request cannot require the recipient to provide information beyond an email address
- The opt-out cannot require the recipient to visit more than a single page to opt out (other than a confirmation page)

### 6. Honor Opt-Out Requests Promptly
Once a recipient opts out, the sender must stop sending commercial email to that address **within 10 business days**. (15 U.S.C. Section 7704(a)(4))
- The sender **cannot transfer or sell** the email address of a person who has opted out, except to a compliance service provider
- The opt-out applies to **all commercial email** from that sender, not just a particular type

### 7. Monitor What Others Do on Your Behalf
Even if a company hires another entity to handle its email marketing, the originating company **remains liable** for compliance. Both the company whose product is promoted and the company that sends the message can be held legally responsible. (15 U.S.C. Section 7704(a)(1))

## Penalties

- Each separate email in violation constitutes a **separate offense** subject to penalties of up to **$51,744** per violation (as adjusted for inflation).
- Additional penalties apply for aggravated violations, including harvesting email addresses, dictionary attacks, and using automated means to register for multiple email accounts.
- Criminal penalties (including imprisonment) apply for certain violations involving fraud, unauthorized computer access, or falsified registration information.

## Preemption (15 U.S.C. Section 7707)

CAN-SPAM **preempts** state laws that regulate the use of electronic mail to send commercial messages, except:
- State laws that prohibit **falsity or deception** in commercial email
- State **computer crime** statutes
- Other state laws not **specifically** addressing commercial email

## Relevance to Desata

### Beta Outreach and Sales Emails
- All commercial emails to prospective institutional clients (financial aid directors, compliance staff) must comply with CAN-SPAM
- Each email must include: accurate header info, truthful subject line, ad identification (unless prior consent), physical address, and an opt-out mechanism
- Opt-out requests must be honored within 10 business days

### Transactional Email Exemption
- Emails to existing beta participants about product updates, account information, or service changes are likely transactional and exempt from most requirements (but must not have false headers)

### Third-Party Email Services
- If Desata uses a third-party email service or marketing platform, Desata remains liable for CAN-SPAM compliance
- Email lists purchased or obtained from third parties do not relieve Desata of compliance obligations
