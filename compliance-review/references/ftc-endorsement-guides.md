# FTC Endorsement Guides — 16 C.F.R. Part 255

**Citation:** 16 C.F.R. Part 255 — Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising
**Source:** https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-255
**Date:** Revised June 29, 2023 (effective July 26, 2023). Originally issued 1980; previously revised 2009.
**Federal Register:** 88 FR 48092 (July 26, 2023)

---

## Overview

The FTC Endorsement Guides are administrative interpretations of Section 5 of the FTC Act (15 U.S.C. 45), which prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices. The Guides apply to endorsements and testimonials in advertising, including online and social media contexts. While not independently enforceable regulations, practices inconsistent with the Guides may result in FTC enforcement under Section 5.

## Key Definitions

### Endorsement (Section 255.0)

An **endorsement** means any advertising, marketing, or promotional message that consumers are likely to believe reflects the opinions, beliefs, findings, or experiences of a party other than the sponsoring advertiser, even if the views expressed by that party are identical to those of the sponsoring advertiser.

The 2023 revision expanded this definition to cover:
- **Fake reviews** — fabricated consumer endorsements
- **Virtual influencers** — AI-generated or fictional endorsers
- **Tags in social media** — tagging a brand can constitute an endorsement if it serves a promotional function

### Clear and Conspicuous (Section 255.0, added 2023)

A disclosure is **clear and conspicuous** if it is difficult to miss (i.e., easily noticeable) and easily understandable by ordinary consumers. A platform's built-in disclosure tool (e.g., Instagram's "Paid partnership" label) may not be sufficient if consumers don't notice or understand it.

## Key Provisions

### Section 255.1 — General Considerations

- An endorsement must reflect the **honest opinions, findings, beliefs, or experience** of the endorser.
- An endorsement may not convey any express or implied representation that would be **deceptive** if made directly by the advertiser.
- If the advertiser does not have **substantiation** that the endorser's experience represents what consumers will generally achieve, the endorsement must clearly and conspicuously disclose either what the generally expected performance would be or the limited applicability of the endorser's experience.

### Section 255.2 — Consumer Endorsements

- An advertisement employing an endorsement reflecting the experience of an individual or a group of consumers on a central or key attribute will be interpreted as representing that the experience is representative of what consumers will generally achieve.
- If the advertiser does not have adequate substantiation, the ad must clearly disclose the generally expected performance or that the endorser's experience is not typical.

### Section 255.3 — Expert Endorsements

- Whenever an advertisement represents, directly or by implication, that the endorser is an **expert**, the endorser's qualifications must give the endorser the expertise to support the endorsement.
- The expert must have actually **examined or tested** the product (where the endorsement implies such evaluation).
- An expert's endorsement must be supported by an actual exercise of that expertise in evaluating the product.

### Section 255.4 — Endorsements by Organizations

- Endorsements by organizations (including educational institutions) are viewed as representing the judgment of a group whose collective experience exceeds that of any individual.
- The organization must have **conducted an evaluation** of the endorsed product sufficient to support the endorsement.

### Section 255.5 — Disclosure of Material Connections

This is the provision most relevant to Desata's operations.

When there is a **connection between the endorser and the seller** of the advertised product that:
1. Might **materially affect the weight or credibility** of the endorsement, AND
2. Would **not reasonably be expected** by the audience

...the connection must be **clearly and conspicuously disclosed**.

**Material connections include:**
- Business or employment relationships
- Family or personal relationships
- Monetary payment (including to intermediaries)
- Free or discounted products (including products unrelated to the endorsed product)
- Other benefits (early access, prizes, media appearances)
- Any financial or economic interest in the sale

**2023 additions to Section 255.5:**
- **Intermediaries are liable.** An advertiser who uses an intermediary (such as an ad agency, PR firm, or review platform) to disseminate endorsements that it knows or should know are deceptive may be liable.
- **Brand monitoring.** Advertisers should have systems to monitor endorser compliance and take steps to address non-compliance.
- **Employees as endorsers.** Employees who endorse their employer's products must disclose their employment relationship.

## Relevance to Desata

### Institutional Testimonials
If Desata solicits or uses testimonials from financial aid officers or institutions:
- Any material connection (free access, partnership, compensation) must be disclosed
- Testimonials must reflect honest experience and not be misleading about what other institutions would experience
- Desata has a duty to monitor compliance if it facilitates endorsement content

### Marketing Claims
- Any claims about ClarifiED's performance (e.g., compliance rates, time savings) that rely on institutional endorsements must be substantiated
- Expert endorsements from compliance professionals require the expert to have actually evaluated the product

### Comparison Tool Marketing
- If institutions or lenders provide endorsements of ClarifiED's recommendation engine, the no-influence, no-commission business model may reduce (but not eliminate) material connection concerns
- Any financial relationship between Desata and an endorsing institution or lender must be disclosed
