# CFPB Circular 2022-03: Adverse Action Notification Requirements and Algorithmic Decision-Making

**Citation:** CFPB Circular 2022-03
**Source:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau
**Fetched from:** consumerfinance.gov

---

## Core Position

The CFPB clarifies that creditors using complex algorithms — including AI and machine learning — must still comply with Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) requirements to disclose specific reasons for adverse credit actions. The circular directly addresses the "black box" problem.

## Key Requirements

**Disclosure Obligation:** Creditors must provide "a statement of specific reasons" to applicants denied credit or experiencing adverse actions, regardless of technology used.

**Specificity Standard:** Statements cannot reference internal standards, policies, or scoring system failure. Instead, creditors must disclose "the specific principal reason(s) for the adverse action."

## The Algorithm Problem

The CFPB explicitly rejects the argument that algorithmic complexity excuses compliance:

> "A creditor cannot justify noncompliance...based on the mere fact that the technology...is too complicated or opaque to understand. A creditor's lack of understanding of its own methods is therefore not a cognizable defense."

## Regulatory Framework

- **Regulation B requirement:** Reasons must "relate to and accurately describe the factors actually considered or scored"
- **Credit scoring systems:** No principal reason may be excluded from disclosure
- **Post-hoc explanations:** While some use approximation methods, creditors must validate accuracy

## Enforcement Implication

Technological opacity provides no legal shelter. Creditors bear responsibility for understanding their own decision systems sufficiently to meet disclosure obligations.

## Relevance to Desata

If ClarifiED's recommendation algorithm produces different results for different borrowers, this circular signals that CFPB may view this as requiring adverse action notices. Key questions:
- Does ClarifiED's algorithm constitute "participation in a credit decision"?
- If a borrower is shown some lenders but not others, is that an adverse action?
- Can the methodology be explained sufficiently to meet the specificity standard?
