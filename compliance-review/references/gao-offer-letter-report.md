# GAO Report GAO-23-104708 — College Financial Aid Offers

**Citation:** U.S. Government Accountability Office, "Financial Aid Offers: Action Needed to Improve Information on College Costs and Student Aid," GAO-23-104708
**Source:** https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-104708
**Full Report PDF:** https://www.gao.gov/assets/gao-23-104708.pdf
**Date:** Published November 1, 2022. Publicly released December 5, 2022.
**Blog Summary:** https://www.gao.gov/blog/what-financial-aid-offers-dont-tell-you-about-cost-college

---

## Overview

The GAO examined how colleges and universities present cost and financial aid information in their financial aid offer letters. The report found widespread deficiencies: most institutions fail to follow best practices, making it difficult for students to compare offers across schools and assess true college affordability.

The report recommended that **Congress consider legislation** requiring standardized, clear financial aid offer information.

## Methodology

The GAO:
- Reviewed financial aid offers from a **nationally representative sample of colleges**
- Compared offers against **10 best practices** for clear and standard information
- Analyzed the use of the Department of Education's College Financing Plan (Shopping Sheet) template
- Interviewed students, financial aid administrators, and other stakeholders

## Key Findings

### 1. Net Price Failures

**About 91% of colleges understate or do not include the net price in their offers.**

Net price — the amount a student will actually pay after subtracting grants and scholarships from the cost of attendance — is the single most important figure for students comparing offers. Yet the vast majority of institutions either:
- Do not include net price at all
- Calculate it incorrectly (e.g., subtracting loans or work-study from costs, which inflates the apparent affordability)

### 2. Missing Cost Information

**29% of colleges do not itemize any costs in their aid offers.**

These institutions present a financial aid "package" without showing what the student is actually paying for. Students receive a letter telling them how much aid they will get, but not what the total cost is — making the aid figure meaningless for decision-making.

### 3. Low Adherence to Best Practices

**None of the colleges in the sample followed all 10 best practices. Two-thirds followed half or fewer.**

The 10 best practices the GAO evaluated include:
- Including all key costs (tuition, fees, housing, meals, books, transportation)
- Itemizing costs
- Separating grants/scholarships from loans and work-study
- Including net price
- Using clear terminology
- Not presenting loans as "awards"

### 4. Minimal Use of Federal Template

**Only about 3% of colleges used the College Financing Plan (Shopping Sheet) as their primary communication to students.**

About two-thirds of colleges did not use the federal template at all. The template was voluntary, and most institutions preferred their own formats — which often lacked key information.

### 5. Confusing Terminology and Presentation

Common problems identified:
- **Loans presented as "awards"** — making borrowed money appear to be free money
- **Gift aid lumped together with loans and work-study** in a single "total aid" figure
- **Use of institutional jargon** that students and families do not understand
- **Missing or unclear definitions** of terms used in the offer

### 6. Inconsistent Definitions Across Schools

The same terms (e.g., "financial aid," "cost of attendance," "net price") were used differently by different institutions, making cross-school comparison nearly impossible for students without expert guidance.

## GAO Recommendations

### To Congress
Congress should consider **requiring institutions** to include clear, standard information in all financial aid offers. The GAO specifically recommended legislation because:
- Voluntary efforts (the College Financing Plan, NASFAA guidelines) have not achieved widespread adoption
- The Department of Education lacks authority to mandate a specific format
- Only Congress can create a legal requirement

### To the Department of Education
The GAO recommended that ED take steps to improve its existing voluntary tools and guidance, though the report acknowledged that without legislative authority, ED's ability to enforce standards is limited.

## Congressional and Industry Response

Following this report:
- Multiple bills were introduced to mandate standardized offer letters (Understanding the True Cost of College Act and others)
- The **College Cost Transparency Initiative (CCT)** was launched in fall 2022 by NASFAA and 10 higher education associations, creating voluntary principles and standards
- As of September 2024, more than 615 institutions have joined the CCT
- No legislation mandating a standardized format has been enacted as of this writing
