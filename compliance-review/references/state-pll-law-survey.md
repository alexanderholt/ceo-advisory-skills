# State Preferred Lender List (PLL) Law Survey

**Purpose:** Identify every state known to have PLL-specific legislation beyond the federal HEOA requirements.
**Last reviewed:** March 2026

---

## Federal Baseline: HEOA (2008)

The Higher Education Opportunity Act of 2008 established federal PLL requirements at 34 C.F.R. Part 601:
- Institutions maintaining a PLL must include at least 3 unaffiliated lenders for FFEL (and by practice, private loans)
- Must disclose selection criteria and methodology
- Must state that borrowers may choose any lender
- Must develop, publish, and enforce a code of conduct
- Annual update required
- Revenue sharing prohibited
- Gift bans with limited exceptions

All Title IV institutions are subject to these requirements regardless of state. The state laws below impose **additional** requirements.

## States with PLL-Specific Legislation

### Tier 1: Comprehensive State PLL Laws

| State | Citation | Year | Key Difference from Federal HEOA |
|-------|----------|------|----------------------------------|
| **New York** | Education Law §§679-b through 679-g (SLATE Act) | 2007 | Strictest state law. Explicit best-interest standard; absolute revenue-sharing ban; gift prohibition broader than federal; anti-steering provision; quid pro quo ban (no cross-product deal-making); font-size matching requirement for borrower choice notice; AG enforcement with active history. See `ny-slate-act.md` for full analysis. |
| **Connecticut** | Conn. Gen. Stat. §10a-223a et seq. | 2007-2008 | Enacted in response to AG investigation. Requires code of conduct; bans gifts and revenue sharing; requires disclosure of lender selection process; prohibits steering. AG enforcement authority. |
| **Minnesota** | Minn. Stat. §136A.61 et seq. | 2007 | Private loan lender code of conduct requirements; prohibits steering; bans gifts and incentives from lenders; requires disclosure of PLL selection criteria. |
| **Pennsylvania** | 24 Pa. Stat. §2510-301 et seq. | 2008 | Enacted concurrently with HEOA. Requires code of conduct; bans revenue sharing; bans gifts; prohibits staffing arrangements; requires PLL disclosures. |
| **Illinois** | 110 ILCS 990/1 et seq. (Student Loan Servicing Rights Act) | 2007-2008 | Code of conduct requirements; prohibits preferred lender arrangements that are not in students' best interest; disclosure requirements; AG enforcement. |
| **New Jersey** | N.J. Stat. §18A:3B-45.1 et seq. | 2008 | Code of conduct requirements; bans revenue sharing; bans gifts; requires annual PLL review. |

### Tier 2: States with Partial PLL Provisions or Related Laws

| State | Citation | Year | Notes |
|-------|----------|------|-------|
| **California** | Cal. Educ. Code §§69996-69997 | 2008 | Requires institutions to provide information about federal and private loans; disclosure requirements for PLLs; less comprehensive than NY or CT. |
| **Maryland** | Md. Code, Educ. §§11-301 et seq. | 2008 | Code of conduct requirements; anti-steering provisions; disclosure requirements. |
| **Massachusetts** | Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 15A, §24 et seq. | 2008 | Requires code of conduct for public institutions; prohibits conflicts of interest; disclosure requirements. |
| **Iowa** | Iowa Code §261G | 2008 | Student loan borrower bill of rights; includes PLL-related disclosure requirements. |
| **Oregon** | ORS §348.594 et seq. | 2008 | Requires code of conduct; disclosure requirements for institutions maintaining PLLs. |
| **Washington** | RCW 28B.90 | 2008 | Code of conduct requirements; disclosure provisions for preferred lender arrangements. |

### Tier 3: States Relying Primarily on Federal HEOA

Most remaining states do not have state-specific PLL legislation and rely on the federal HEOA framework at 34 C.F.R. Part 601. Institutions in these states are still bound by the federal requirements, which are enforced through participation agreements with ED.

## Common State Requirements Beyond Federal HEOA

The state laws listed above generally share these additional elements:
1. **State AG enforcement** (federal HEOA relies on ED enforcement through institutional participation agreements)
2. **Broader gift bans** (federal HEOA has some safe harbors for certain food, training materials, etc.)
3. **More prescriptive disclosure requirements** (specific font, placement, and language mandates)
4. **Explicit anti-steering provisions** with state-law penalties
5. **Requirements extending to private institutions** (some state laws cover institutions that might not be covered by certain federal provisions)
