# FTC .com Disclosures Guidance

**Citation:** Federal Trade Commission, ".com Disclosures: How to Make Effective Disclosures in Digital Advertising" (March 2013)
**Source:** https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/documents/plain-language/bus41-dot-com-disclosures-information-about-online-advertising.pdf
**Date:** March 12, 2013 (updated from original 2000 guidance). Principles remain current as of 2026.
**FTC Press Release:** https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2013/03/ftc-staff-revises-online-advertising-disclosure-guidelines

## Overview

This FTC staff guidance document explains how consumer protection law applies to online advertising. Its central principle: if a disclosure is necessary to prevent an ad from being deceptive or unfair, the disclosure must be **clear and conspicuous**. The guidance applies the same consumer protection principles that govern offline advertising, adapted for digital media including websites, email, and mobile.

Note: The FTC announced in 2022 that it was revisiting this guidance (FTC-2022-0035). As of this writing, the 2013 version remains the current published guidance.

## Core Principle

**If a disclosure is needed to prevent an online ad claim from being deceptive or unfair, the disclosure must be clear and conspicuous.**

This applies across all devices and platforms that consumers may use to view the ad — desktop, mobile, tablet, email, social media, and apps.

## Key Requirements

### 1. Proximity and Placement

- Disclosures must be **close to the claim** they qualify. The closer the disclosure is to the triggering claim, the more likely consumers are to notice and understand it.
- On a webpage, a disclosure should be placed **on the same page** and near the relevant claim, not on a separate page.
- Disclosures should appear **before** or **at the same time** as the claim, not after the consumer has acted on the claim.
- Disclosures that appear only on a "terms and conditions" page, accessible only through a link, are likely **insufficient**.

### 2. Prominence

- Disclosures must be **displayed prominently** — in a font size, color, and style that makes them noticeable.
- A disclosure in small, gray text against a white background is less likely to be noticed than one in the same size and color as the surrounding text.
- **Contrast** with the background matters.
- Disclosures should not be buried in a block of text or lost in a cluttered visual environment.

### 3. Avoiding Distractions

- Graphics, animations, sound, links, or other elements that **draw the consumer's attention away** from a disclosure may render it ineffective.
- Flashing images or animated content near a disclosure can reduce its prominence.
- Do not use elements that cause consumers to click away before they see the disclosure.

### 4. Hyperlinks

- Hyperlinks **should not be used** to convey disclosures about product cost or certain health and safety issues. These disclosures must appear on the same page as the claim.
- Where hyperlinks are appropriate for other types of disclosures:
  - The hyperlink label should be **specific** (not "click here" or "details" — use language like "see important pricing information")
  - The link should be **conspicuous** through font, size, color, or position
  - The link should take consumers **directly to the disclosure**, not to an intermediate page
  - The disclosure page should be **consistent** with the hyperlink label

### 5. Repetition

- If a claim appears in multiple places on a website or in an email, the disclosure may need to be repeated so consumers see it regardless of where they encounter the claim.

### 6. Multimedia (Audio/Video)

- In audio or video formats, disclosures must be presented in the **same medium** as the claim (i.e., an audio claim requires an audio disclosure).
- Disclosures should be of **sufficient duration** to be noticed and understood.
- Superimposed text in video must remain on-screen long enough to be read.

### 7. Mobile-Specific Guidance

- Disclosures must be effective on **small screens**. If a claim triggers a required disclosure and that disclosure cannot be made clearly and conspicuously on a particular device, **do not make the claim on that device**.
- Scrolling can bury disclosures — design for disclosure visibility without scrolling where possible.
- Pop-up disclosures may not work on devices that block pop-ups.
- Consider screen orientation (portrait vs. landscape) when designing disclosures.

## "Clear and Conspicuous" Evaluation Factors

The FTC evaluates whether a disclosure is clear and conspicuous by looking at:

1. **Placement** — Is the disclosure where consumers will actually look?
2. **Proximity** — Is it near the claim it modifies?
3. **Prominence** — Is it big enough and visible enough?
4. **No-distractions** — Is it free from competing elements?
5. **Repetition** — Does it appear wherever the claim appears?
6. **Medium** — Is it in the same medium as the claim?
7. **Understandability** — Can ordinary consumers understand it?

## Relevance to Desata

### Product Websites (desata.io, loanlimit.app, beta.loanmatch.app)
- Any marketing claims about compliance, savings, or institutional benefits must have necessary disclosures placed clearly on the same page
- The no-influence, no-commission model is a marketing claim — any qualifications must be conspicuous
- Methodology disclosures should not be hidden behind generic hyperlinks

### Email Marketing
- Commercial emails making product claims must include required disclosures within the email, not solely on a linked landing page
- Disclosures must be visible in both desktop and mobile email clients

### Comparison Tool / Recommendation Engine
- If ClarifiED presents loan options, any disclosures about how recommendations are generated, what data is used, or what factors influence ordering must be clear and conspicuous on the same screen as the recommendations
- If the tool is used on small screens, disclosures must still be effective

## Notes on Completeness

This summary covers the key principles and requirements from the 2013 guidance. The full document contains detailed examples and illustrations not reproduced here. The FTC has signaled it may update this guidance but has not yet published a revision.
